# Stack Power

Stack power is the total electrical power generated by a microbial fuel cell stack or consumed by a microbial electrolysis cell stack, calculated as the product of stack voltage and stack current (P = V x I). In MFCs, stack power represents the net bioelectrochemical energy harvested from organic substrates, while in MECs, it represents the electrical energy input required to drive hydrogen production or other cathodic reactions beyond their thermodynamic equilibrium. Stack power is the primary performance metric for evaluating the energy viability of MES technology at scale and directly determines the economic feasibility of power generation or the energy cost of product synthesis.

**Power density benchmarks (normalized to electrode area):**
- Lab-scale MFC maximum: 2 - 7 W/m^2 (fed-batch, optimized conditions)
- Pilot-scale MFC sustained: 0.05 - 0.5 W/m^2 (continuous, real wastewater)
- MEC energy input: 0.5 - 5 W/m^2 (to achieve practical H2 production rates)

## Measurement Methods

- **1. **Direct power calculation**: Measure voltage across external load and current through the circuit simultaneously. P = V_load x I. Use time-averaged values for fluctuating biological systems.**: 2. **Polarization curve method**: Sweep the external resistance from open circuit to near short circuit while recording voltage and current. Plot power (P = V x I) vs. current to find the maximum power point. 3. **Maximum power point tracking (MPPT)**: Use electronic MPPT circuits (perturb-and-observe, incremental conductance) to continuously operate at the maximum power point and log power output. 4. **Energy harvesting circuit measurement**: Measure input and output of power management circuits (charge pumps, boost converters, capacitor-based harvesters) to determine usable power after conversion losses. 5. **Calorimetric validation**: For MECs, compare electrical energy input with chemical energy output (hydrogen heating value) to validate energy balance and identify losses.

## Affecting Factors

- **text**: - **Number of cells and arrangement**: Series connections increase voltage; parallel connections increase current. Maximum power requires matched arrangement for the intended load.
- **Internal resistance**: Total stack internal resistance limits maximum power. At maximum power transfer, external load equals internal resistance (P_max = OCV^2 / 4R_int).
- **Substrate loading**: Insufficient organic substrate limits current generation and thus power. Overloading can cause methanogenic competition, also reducing power.
- **Temperature**: Power output increases with temperature up to the mesophilic optimum (30-37 C), then declines at thermophilic temperatures due to biofilm stress.
- **Membrane condition**: Ion exchange membrane fouling and degradation increase internal resistance, reducing power by 20-50% over months of operation.
- **Power conditioning losses**: DC-DC converters, charge pumps, and inverters introduce 10-40% energy conversion losses between gross and usable power.
- **Electrode degradation**: Catalyst deactivation, biofilm detachment, or corrosion reduce power output over time (typical degradation: 1-5% per month in pilot systems).

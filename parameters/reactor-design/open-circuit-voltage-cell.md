# Open Circuit Voltage Cell

Open circuit voltage (OCV) is the maximum thermodynamic voltage that an individual microbial electrochemical system (MES) cell can produce when no current is flowing through the external circuit (infinite external resistance). The OCV represents the difference in electrochemical potential between the anode and cathode under equilibrium conditions and serves as the theoretical upper limit for the cell's operating voltage. In microbial fuel cells (MFCs), the OCV is generated by the potential difference between the microbial oxidation of organic substrates at the anode and the reduction of an electron acceptor (typically oxygen) at the cathode.

The theoretical maximum OCV for an MFC oxidizing acetate with an air cathode is approximately 1.1 V (calculated from the Nernst equation using standard reduction potentials: E_cathode = +0.805 V vs. SHE for O2/H2O at pH 7, and E_anode = -0.296 V vs. SHE for CO2/acetate at pH 7). However, practical OCVs are always lower than this theoretical maximum due to mixed potentials at the electrodes, oxygen crossover from cathode to anode, substrate crossover from anode to cathode, internal current leakage, and non-equilibrium conditions at the biofilm. Typical measured OCVs range from 0.6 to 0.8 V for well-functioning MFCs.

For microbial electrolysis cells (MECs), the OCV is negative because the hydrogen evolution reaction at the cathode has a more negative reduction potential than the anode reaction, requiring an externally applied voltage to drive the reaction. The minimum applied voltage for MEC operation (the theoretical thermodynamic requirement) is approximately 0.14 V for acetate oxidation coupled to hydrogen evolution at pH 7, though practical systems require 0.2--0.8 V due to overpotentials. In MDCs, the OCV reflects the combined bioelectrochemical and Donnan (membrane) potentials and is typically 0.5--0.8 V.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 0--2 V (MFC); negative for MEC (required applied voltage)
- **Typical Range**: 0.5--0.9 V (MFC)
- **Theoretical maximum (MFC, acetate/O2)**: ~1.1 V
- **Well-optimized air-cathode MFC**: 0.7--0.85 V
- **Dual-chamber MFC with ferricyanide cathode**: 0.75--0.85 V
- **H-type MFC**: 0.6--0.8 V
- **MFC with wastewater**: 0.5--0.7 V (lower due to mixed substrates)
- **MDC**: 0.5--0.8 V
- **MEC (minimum applied voltage)**: 0.14 V theoretical; 0.2--0.8 V practical
- **Sediment MFC**: 0.4--0.7 V
- **Single-chamber MFC (after O2 crossover losses)**: 0.6--0.75 V

## Measurement Methods

- **Direct Voltage Measurement (Standard Method)**: Disconnect the external load (or set the potentiostat to open circuit mode) and measure the voltage between the anode and cathode terminals using a high-impedance voltmeter (input impedance greater than 10 Mohm to minimize current draw). Allow the voltage to stabilize for 30--60 minutes after disconnecting the load, as the voltage will gradually increase from the operating voltage to the OCV as the electrode potentials re-equilibrate. Record the stabilized OCV value. Measure against a reference electrode (Ag/AgCl) to determine individual electrode potentials.
- **Potentiostat Open Circuit Measurement**: Use a potentiostat in open circuit potential (OCP) mode to continuously record the OCV over time. This provides a precise time-resolved measurement of the OCV and can reveal the approach to equilibrium. Modern potentiostats have input impedances exceeding 10^12 ohm, ensuring negligible current draw. Record both the full-cell OCV and individual electrode potentials vs. a reference electrode.
- **Nernst Equation Calculation**: Calculate the theoretical OCV from the Nernst equation: OCV = E_cathode - E_anode, where each electrode potential is E = E_standard - (RT/nF) * ln(Q), with R = gas constant, T = temperature, n = electrons transferred, F = Faraday constant, and Q = reaction quotient. This requires knowledge of substrate concentration, product concentration, pH, temperature, and dissolved oxygen concentration. Compare the calculated value with the measured OCV to assess electrode performance.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Substrate Type and Concentration**: Different substrates have different standard potentials. Acetate (-0.296 V vs. SHE at pH 7) is the most common reference. Higher substrate concentrations increase the OCV slightly through the Nernst equation.
- **Cathode Electron Acceptor**: Oxygen (+0.805 V at pH 7) gives the highest OCV; ferricyanide (+0.361 V) is commonly used in lab studies; hydrogen evolution (-0.414 V at pH 7) requires applied voltage (MEC).
- **pH**: Each pH unit deviation from 7 shifts the electrode potential by approximately 59 mV at 25 C. Anode acidification and cathode alkalinization both reduce the OCV.
- **Oxygen Crossover**: In membraneless designs, oxygen diffusion from cathode to anode reduces the OCV by establishing a mixed potential at the anode (loss of 50--200 mV).
- **Temperature**: OCV increases slightly with temperature (about 1 mV/C) through improved kinetics and Nernst effects.
- **Biofilm Maturity**: A mature electroactive biofilm establishes a lower (more negative) anode potential, increasing the OCV. Startup OCVs may be 100--200 mV lower than mature values.
- **Internal Leakage Current**: Even at open circuit, small internal currents (from substrate crossover, electron shuttles, or membrane conductivity) prevent true equilibrium.
- **Dissolved Oxygen at Anode**: Any dissolved oxygen in the anode chamber scavenges electrons and reduces the OCV.

## Related Parameters

- **name**: Operating Voltage (Cell)

- **relationship**: V_operating = OCV - I * R_int; always less than OCV under load.
- **name**: Internal Resistance (Cell)

- **relationship**: Determines the voltage drop from OCV to operating voltage at a given current.
- **name**: Power Density (Cell)

- **relationship**: Maximum power = OCV^2 / (4 * R_int).
- **name**: Current Density (Cell)

- **relationship**: Maximum theoretical current = OCV / R_int.
- **name**: pH Setpoint

- **relationship**: Affects both electrode potentials and thus the OCV.
- **name**: Temperature Setpoint

- **relationship**: Influences OCV through thermodynamic and kinetic effects.
- **name**: Stack Voltage

- **relationship**: OCV_stack = n_cells * OCV_cell for series connection.

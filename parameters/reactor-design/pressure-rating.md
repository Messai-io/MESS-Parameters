# Pressure Rating

Pressure rating is the maximum internal gauge pressure that a microbial electrochemical system (MES) reactor can safely withstand during normal operation, expressed in bar, kPa, or psi. This parameter is determined by the structural strength of the weakest component in the pressure boundary---typically the reactor body walls, gaskets, membrane, or fittings---and must include an appropriate safety factor (typically 2--4x the expected maximum operating pressure). The pressure rating is particularly critical for microbial electrolysis cells (MECs), where hydrogen gas accumulation in sealed chambers can generate significant pressures if gas collection is interrupted or insufficient.

For most MFC applications, the internal pressure is near atmospheric (the reactor is either open to the atmosphere through the air cathode or sealed with minimal headspace pressure). However, pressurized operation may be deliberately employed to increase dissolved gas concentrations, suppress gas bubble formation, or facilitate gas compression for storage. In MECs, the cathode chamber must withstand the pressure generated by hydrogen accumulation, which can reach several bar if the gas collection system fails or is undersized. The pressure rating must account for both normal operating pressures and potential fault conditions (blocked gas outlet, pump failure, temperature excursion).

The pressure rating also affects the choice of reactor materials, wall thickness, gasket design, and membrane support structure. Higher pressure ratings require thicker walls, stronger materials, more robust gaskets, and potentially membrane support plates to prevent membrane extrusion or rupture. For pilot-scale and industrial MES systems, pressure vessels may need to comply with local pressure vessel codes (ASME BPVC in the US, PED in Europe) depending on the volume and pressure rating.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 1--50 bar (absolute)
- **Typical Range**: 1--5 bar (absolute); 0--4 bar gauge
- **Open-air MFC (air-cathode)**: 1 bar (atmospheric; not pressurized)
- **Sealed MFC (gas analysis)**: 1.0--1.5 bar absolute (slightly above atmospheric due to gas generation)
- **MEC (laboratory)**: 1--3 bar gauge (designed for H2 accumulation)
- **MEC (pilot-scale)**: 1--10 bar gauge (for pressurized H2 collection)
- **MDC**: 1--3 bar gauge
- **Microfluidic MFC**: 1--5 bar (to overcome channel pressure drop)
- **Pressurized MEC for direct H2 compression**: 10--50 bar
- **Safety factor**: Typically 2x normal operating pressure; 4x for public safety

## Measurement Methods

- **Hydrostatic Pressure Test**: Fill the reactor with water (no gas headspace) and pressurize using a hydraulic pump while monitoring with a calibrated pressure gauge. Increase pressure gradually in increments of 10% of the expected rating. The test pressure is typically 1.5x the rated working pressure (per ASME standards). Hold the test pressure for a minimum of 30 minutes and inspect for leaks, deformation, or failure. No visible leaks or permanent deformation indicates a passing test.
- **Burst Pressure Testing**: Apply increasing pressure to a sacrificial reactor unit until failure occurs. The burst pressure divided by the safety factor gives the working pressure rating. This destructive test is performed during design validation, not during production testing. Typical safety factors are 3--4x for polymer reactors and 2--3x for metallic reactors.
- **Finite Element Analysis (FEA)**: Create a 3D structural model of the reactor in FEA software (ANSYS, SolidWorks Simulation, or COMSOL). Apply internal pressure loading and evaluate the stress distribution, maximum stress locations, and deformation. Compare the maximum stress with the material's yield stress and apply the appropriate safety factor. This method can identify stress concentration points (e.g., at corners, port holes, or gasket grooves) that limit the overall pressure rating.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Cell Material**: Material yield strength directly determines the pressure capability. Acrylic (yield ~70 MPa) is weaker than polycarbonate (yield ~62 MPa) which is weaker than stainless steel 316L (yield ~205 MPa).
- **Wall Thickness**: Pressure rating increases approximately linearly with wall thickness for cylindrical vessels (hoop stress formula: sigma = P * r / t, where r is radius and t is wall thickness).
- **Reactor Shape**: Cylindrical shapes are inherently stronger under internal pressure than rectangular shapes, which experience bending stress in flat walls. Spherical shapes are strongest.
- **Gasket Performance**: Gasket material, compression, and contact area determine the maximum sealing pressure. Typical elastomer gaskets seal to 5--10 bar; metal gaskets can seal to 100+ bar.
- **Temperature**: Material strength decreases at elevated temperatures. Polymer reactor walls lose 20--50% of their strength between 20 C and 60 C.
- **Fatigue**: Repeated pressure cycling (from batch operation, gas collection, or flow pulsation) can cause fatigue failure at pressures well below the static rating.
- **Chemical Degradation**: Long-term exposure to electrolytes can reduce material strength through chemical attack, swelling, or stress cracking.
- **Port Holes and Penetrations**: Each hole in the pressure boundary (for tubing, electrodes, sensors) creates a stress concentration that reduces the effective pressure rating.
- **Membrane Strength**: Ion exchange membranes are typically the weakest pressure boundary component, rated for 0.5--3 bar differential pressure depending on the type and support structure.

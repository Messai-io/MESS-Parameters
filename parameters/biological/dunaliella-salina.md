# Dunaliella Salina

*Dunaliella salina* is a unicellular, halotolerant green microalga (Chlorophyceae) that thrives in hypersaline environments such as salt lakes, salt pans, and coastal lagoons. It is best known for its extraordinary ability to accumulate massive quantities of beta-carotene (up to 14% of dry weight) under stress conditions, making it the world's primary commercial source of natural beta-carotene. In the context of microbial electrochemical systems, *D. salina* has attracted interest for photosynthetic MFC applications, algal-assisted biocathodes, and integrated biorefinery concepts that couple MES with high-value product extraction.

In photosynthetic MFCs (photo-MFCs), *D. salina* serves as the biocatalyst in the cathode chamber, where photosynthetic oxygen evolution provides the cathodic electron acceptor (O2) that completes the MFC circuit without the need for mechanical aeration. The alga can also function in the anode chamber of specially designed systems, where organic substrates exuded during photosynthesis and dark respiration support exoelectrogenic communities. Furthermore, algal biomass from *D. salina* cultivation can serve as a feedstock for MFC anodes after lipid extraction, creating a valorization pathway for residual biomass.

The halotolerance of *D. salina* (growing in 0.5-5 M NaCl) provides a unique advantage for MES treating saline wastewaters, including desalination brines, aquaculture effluents, and oil and gas produced waters. High-salinity media also exhibit superior ionic conductivity, reducing internal resistance in MES. The organism lacks a rigid cell wall (protected instead by a glycocalyx), which facilitates nutrient exchange and may enhance interactions with electrode surfaces in bioelectrochemical configurations.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range (beta-carotene content)**: 0.5 to 14% of dry weight
- **Typical Range (beta-carotene content)**: 2 to 10% of dry weight
- **Growth Rate**: 0.1 to 0.5 day-1 (specific growth rate)
- **Doubling Time**: 1.5 to 7 days
- **Salinity Tolerance**: 0.5 to 5.0 M NaCl
- **Optimal Temperature**: 20-30 degrees C
- **Optimal Light Intensity**: 200-400 umol photons/m2/s
- **Photosynthetic O2 Production**: 50-200 mg O2/L/day in dense cultures
- **Photo-MFC Power Density**: 5-50 mW/m2 (cathode limited)
- **Biomass Productivity**: 0.1-0.5 g/L/day in open ponds

## Measurement Methods

- **Spectrophotometric Growth Monitoring**: Cell density is monitored by absorbance at 680 nm (chlorophyll a peak) or 450 nm (carotenoid peak). The ratio of A450/A680 provides an indicator of stress-induced carotenogenesis. Calibration curves relating OD to cell counts (hemocytometer or Coulter counter) and dry weight enable quantitative growth tracking.
- **Carotenoid Extraction and HPLC Analysis**: Cells are harvested by centrifugation, lysed in organic solvents (acetone, hexane, or methanol:dichloromethane), and carotenoids are separated by HPLC with photodiode array detection. Beta-carotene (all-trans and 9-cis isomers) is quantified against authenticated standards. This characterizes the high-value coproduct potential of integrated MES-algal systems.
- **Dissolved Oxygen Measurements**: Clark-type oxygen electrodes or optical dissolved oxygen sensors measure O2 production rates in illuminated algal suspensions and consumption rates in the dark. Net and gross photosynthetic rates are determined from light-dark shift experiments. In photo-MFC cathodes, oxygen availability directly determines cathodic performance.
- **Electrochemical Characterization of Photo-MFCs**: Photo-MFCs with *D. salina* biocathodes are characterized by polarization curves under illuminated and dark conditions. The difference reveals the photosynthetic contribution to cathodic oxygen supply. Chronoamperometry tracks current production over diel light-dark cycles. Power curves distinguish between anodic and cathodic limitations.
- **Chlorophyll Fluorescence (PAM Fluorometry)**: Pulse-amplitude-modulated (PAM) fluorometry measures photosystem II efficiency (Fv/Fm), electron transport rate (ETR), and non-photochemical quenching (NPQ). These parameters assess the photosynthetic health and stress status of *D. salina* in MES applications.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Salinity**: *D. salina* requires NaCl for growth and accumulates glycerol as an osmolyte. Low salinity (<0.5 M NaCl) causes cell lysis. Extreme salinity (>5 M) reduces growth rate. The ionic strength of the growth medium directly affects MES internal resistance, with higher salinity generally beneficial for electrochemical performance.
- **Light Intensity and Photoperiod**: Photosynthesis is light-dependent. Low light limits O2 production and biomass growth. High light (>500 umol/m2/s) can cause photoinhibition but stimulates beta-carotene accumulation. Diel cycles create fluctuating current patterns in photo-MFCs.
- **Nitrogen Availability**: Nitrogen limitation is the primary trigger for beta-carotene overproduction. N-replete conditions favor green, fast-growing cells. N-depleted conditions produce orange, carotenoid-rich cells with slower growth. This creates a trade-off between growth rate and product value.
- **Temperature**: Growth is optimal at 22-28 degrees C. Above 35 degrees C, thermal stress reduces photosynthetic efficiency. Below 15 degrees C, growth slows substantially.
- **CO2 Supply**: Carbon fixation requires CO2. Supplementing cultures with 2-5% CO2 in air increases growth rates 2-5 fold. CO2 from MFC anode chamber (microbial respiration) can be channeled to algal cathode chambers in integrated designs.
- **Mixing and Mass Transfer**: Adequate mixing ensures light distribution, CO2 supply, and nutrient access. Over-mixing can damage the wall-less cells.
- **pH**: *D. salina* tolerates pH 6-10, with optimum around pH 7.5-9.0. Photosynthetic CO2 uptake drives pH upward; CO2 supplementation stabilizes pH.
- **Contamination**: The halophilic niche provides some natural protection against contamination, but protozoan grazers and competing halotolerant algae can reduce culture purity.

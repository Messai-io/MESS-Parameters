# Ultrasound Power

Ultrasound power quantifies the acoustic energy delivered per unit time to microbial electrochemical systems (MES), expressed in watts (W) or as power density in watts per liter (W/L) or watts per square centimeter of transducer face area (W/cm^2). This parameter determines the intensity of acoustic effects including cavitation, acoustic streaming, and microconvection, directly controlling the treatment efficacy for electrode cleaning, biofilm management, and mass transfer enhancement. Ultrasound power is the primary scaling parameter for translating laboratory ultrasonic treatment protocols to pilot and full-scale MES installations.

The relationship between ultrasound power and MES performance follows a biphasic response. At low power (less than 1 W/L), acoustic streaming creates gentle microconvection that enhances substrate diffusion through the biofilm boundary layer, increasing current density by 5-20% without biofilm disruption. At moderate power (1-10 W/L), cavitation onset generates more aggressive mixing and localized mechanical effects that thin the biofilm by removing loosely attached outer layers, further increasing performance by 20-50% through reduced diffusion resistance. At high power (more than 10 W/L), violent cavitation causes extensive biofilm damage, membrane erosion, and electrode surface pitting, decreasing performance despite maximal mass transfer.

The energy efficiency of ultrasonic treatment in MES depends on the ratio of performance improvement to energy input. At optimal ultrasound power (typically 2-5 W/L), the additional electricity generated by the MES due to enhanced mass transfer can partially offset the ultrasonic energy cost, achieving net energy improvement ratios of 1.2-3.0 (additional MES energy output per unit ultrasonic energy input). Above optimal power, the energy ratio decreases below 1.0, making the treatment energetically counterproductive. Below optimal power, the treatment is energetically favorable but may not achieve sufficient biofilm management for long-term operational stability.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 0.001 - 1000 W/L
- **Typical Range for MES**: 0.1 - 50 W/L
- **Gentle acoustic streaming**: 0.1-1 W/L
- **Moderate cavitation**: 1-10 W/L
- **Vigorous cavitation (cleaning)**: 10-100 W/L
- **Industrial ultrasonic cleaning bath**: 20-50 W/L
- **Laboratory probe sonicator (tip)**: 50-500 W/cm^2
- **Transducer face intensity**: 1-10 W/cm^2
- **Energy-neutral threshold for MES enhancement**: approximately 2-5 W/L

## Measurement Methods

- **Calorimetric Method**: The reference method for acoustic power determination measures the temperature rise in a known volume of water subjected to ultrasonic irradiation. Acoustic power P = m * Cp * (dT/dt), where m is water mass (kg), Cp is specific heat (4186 J/kg/K), and dT/dt is temperature rise rate (K/s). The measurement is performed in a well-insulated vessel (Dewar flask or polystyrene container) with continuous stirring and thermistor measurement (0.01 K resolution). Accuracy of 5-10% is achievable with careful attention to thermal losses and stirring effects. This method measures the actual acoustic power absorbed by the medium, inherently accounting for all coupling and transmission losses.
- **Radiation Force Balance**: A precision balance (0.01 mg resolution) supports an acoustic absorber target (conical rubber, castor oil-filled balloon) in the acoustic field. The radiation force F = P/c displaces the target, where P is acoustic power and c is sound speed. For P = 10 W at 25 kHz in water, F = 6.7 mN (equivalent to 0.68 g on the balance). This method provides accuracy of 3-5% and is traceable to SI units through force measurement. It is the IEC 61161 reference standard for ultrasonic power measurement.
- **Electrical Power Monitoring**: The electrical input power to the ultrasonic transducer is measured using a wattmeter on the generator output. Acoustic output power is estimated by multiplying electrical power by the transducer electro-acoustic efficiency (typically 50-85% for PZT transducers at resonance). While less accurate than calorimetric measurement, this approach enables continuous real-time power monitoring during MES treatment. Power factor correction and harmonic content must be considered for accurate measurement of switching-type ultrasonic generators.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Transducer efficiency and coupling**: Piezoelectric transducer efficiency varies from 30-85% depending on frequency, construction, and acoustic loading. Efficiency peaks at the mechanical resonance frequency and drops rapidly off-resonance. Coupling between transducer and reactor (direct immersion, external bonding, water coupling) introduces additional losses of 5-50%. Direct-contact horn transducers achieve the highest delivered power density but are limited in treatment area. Bath-type transducers bonded to reactor walls provide larger treatment areas but lower intensity.
- **Liquid volume and geometry**: Acoustic power density (W/L) scales inversely with reactor volume at constant total power. A 50 W ultrasonic source provides 50 W/L in a 1 L reactor but only 0.5 W/L in a 100 L reactor. Scaling ultrasonic power to maintain constant power density in larger reactors becomes technically challenging and energetically expensive. Multi-transducer arrays with combined power output of 500-5000 W are used for pilot-scale MES applications (100-1000 L), with array design optimization to ensure uniform power distribution.
- **Frequency dependence of power effects**: At constant acoustic power, the biological and electrochemical effects differ dramatically with frequency. At 20 kHz, most power goes into transient cavitation (violent bubble collapse), producing localized extreme conditions. At 500 kHz, the same power produces acoustic streaming and gentle stable cavitation over a larger volume. At 2 MHz, the power is absorbed primarily as heat with negligible mechanical effects. The effective power for biofilm management at 20 kHz is approximately 1/10 to 1/5 of that required at 200 kHz due to the more intense cavitation.
- **Dissolved gas content and static pressure**: Dissolved gas acts as cavitation nuclei, reducing the power threshold for cavitation inception. In MES reactors with active gas production (CO2, H2, CH4), the abundant dissolved gas reduces the effective cavitation threshold, meaning less power is required for a given cavitation effect compared to degassed systems. Increased static pressure (pressurized reactor operation at 2-5 bar) suppresses cavitation, requiring proportionally higher acoustic power to achieve the same effects as atmospheric pressure operation.
- **Temperature**: Water temperature affects acoustic power delivery through changes in viscosity (affecting streaming), surface tension (affecting cavitation threshold), and vapor pressure (affecting cavitation intensity). At temperatures near the boiling point, cavitation bubbles contain more vapor, producing less violent collapse (cushioned by vapor compression). The calorimetric efficiency of power measurement decreases at higher temperatures due to increased thermal losses. Optimal temperature for ultrasonic MES treatment is typically 20-30 degrees C, where cavitation is vigorous and thermal losses are manageable.

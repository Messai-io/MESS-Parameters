# Sound Frequency

Sound frequency defines the oscillation rate of acoustic pressure waves propagating through the liquid and gas phases of microbial electrochemical systems (MES), measured in hertz (Hz). Acoustic stimulation of MES represents an emerging enhancement strategy that exploits the mechanical, thermal, and cavitation effects of sound waves to improve mass transfer, biofilm permeability, electrode surface renewal, and direct biological stimulation of electroactive microorganisms. The frequency determines which physical mechanism dominates the system response, ranging from bulk mixing at low frequencies to cavitation and streaming at ultrasonic frequencies.

In the audible range (20 Hz - 20 kHz), sound waves generate alternating compression and rarefaction in the electrolyte, creating oscillatory fluid motion (acoustic streaming) that enhances mass transfer at electrode-biofilm interfaces. Low-frequency sound (20-500 Hz) produces macro-scale mixing through acoustic streaming velocities of 0.1-10 mm/s, comparable to gentle stirring. Mid-frequency sound (500 Hz - 5 kHz) creates boundary layer disruption at electrode surfaces, thinning the diffusion layer by 20-50% and proportionally increasing mass transfer rates. High-frequency audible sound (5-20 kHz) can induce resonance in biofilm structures, with certain frequencies matching the natural frequency of biofilm streamers and causing enhanced detachment of loosely bound biomass.

At ultrasonic frequencies (above 20 kHz), acoustic cavitation becomes the dominant mechanism. Cavitation bubble collapse generates localized temperatures of 4000-5000 K, pressures of 100-1000 atm, and microjets with velocities of 100-400 m/s, all confined to micrometer-scale regions within microsecond timeframes. These extreme conditions can clean fouled electrode surfaces, disrupt mature biofilms, enhance substrate dissolution, and promote electrochemical reactions. However, the same mechanisms can irreversibly damage sensitive biological components if applied too aggressively.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 1 Hz - 10 MHz
- **Typical Range for MES**: 20 Hz - 100 kHz
- **Low-frequency mixing**: 20-500 Hz
- **Mid-frequency mass transfer**: 500 Hz - 5 kHz
- **Ultrasonic cleaning**: 20-40 kHz
- **High-frequency ultrasound**: 40-200 kHz
- **Diagnostic ultrasound**: 1-20 MHz
- **Biofilm resonance frequencies**: 10 Hz - 1 kHz (structure dependent)
- **Acoustic streaming onset**: approximately 100 Hz (depends on medium and geometry)

## Measurement Methods

- **Hydrophone Measurement**: Calibrated hydrophones (Bruel and Kjaer 8103, Reson TC4013) provide direct measurement of acoustic pressure levels and frequency content in liquid media. Piezoelectric hydrophones with flat frequency response from 1 Hz to 200 kHz measure sound pressure levels from 40 dB re 1 muPa to 220 dB re 1 muPa. The hydrophone is positioned within the MES reactor at locations of interest (near electrodes, in bulk electrolyte) and the signal is analyzed using FFT spectrum analyzers to determine frequency components, harmonic content, and spatial distribution. For ultrasonic frequencies above 200 kHz, needle hydrophones with 0.5 mm active element diameter provide the necessary bandwidth and spatial resolution.
- **Accelerometer Measurement**: Accelerometers mounted on reactor walls or electrode supports measure the structural vibration response to acoustic excitation, providing indirect characterization of the acoustic field. This approach is particularly useful for identifying resonance frequencies of the reactor structure that may amplify or attenuate applied acoustic energy at specific frequencies. MEMS accelerometers (bandwidth to 10 kHz) are suitable for audible-range characterization, while piezoelectric accelerometers extend to ultrasonic frequencies.
- **Acoustic Emission Monitoring**: Passive acoustic emission (AE) sensors detect sounds generated by processes within the MES reactor, including gas bubble formation and collapse, biofilm detachment events, and electrode cracking. AE sensors with 100 kHz - 1 MHz bandwidth detect the high-frequency stress waves generated by these events, enabling real-time monitoring of processes that are otherwise difficult to observe. Bubble departure frequency, for example, correlates with gas production rate and can be monitored acoustically without visual access.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Acoustic source type and power**: Piezoelectric transducers provide efficient energy conversion at specific resonant frequencies (typically 20 kHz, 40 kHz, or custom frequencies) with narrow bandwidth (1-5 kHz). Electromagnetic speakers provide broadband coverage from 20 Hz to 20 kHz with variable frequency control. Magnetostrictive transducers operate at 15-25 kHz with high power capability (100 W - 10 kW). The source determines available frequency range and maximum acoustic intensity deliverable to the MES reactor.
- **Reactor geometry and acoustics**: Standing wave patterns develop when sound wavelength matches reactor dimensions (wavelength = speed of sound / frequency; at 1 kHz in water, wavelength = 1.5 m; at 40 kHz, wavelength = 37 mm). Standing waves create spatial zones of high and low acoustic intensity (nodes and antinodes), with electrode surfaces at antinodes experiencing maximum acoustic effects while those at nodes receive minimal stimulation. Reactor dimensions should be designed to distribute acoustic energy uniformly or to position electrodes at antinodes.
- **Medium properties**: Sound speed in the electrolyte (typically 1450-1550 m/s in water-based MES solutions) depends on temperature (+3 m/s per degree C), salinity (+1.5 m/s per g/L NaCl), and dissolved gas content (-0.5 to -5 m/s per % gas volume). Acoustic attenuation increases with frequency squared in water, limiting penetration depth at high frequencies: at 1 MHz, attenuation is 0.22 dB/cm versus 0.0022 dB/cm at 100 kHz. This frequency-dependent attenuation determines the effective treatment volume.
- **Dissolved gas content**: Dissolved gases dramatically affect acoustic cavitation threshold and intensity. Gas-saturated solutions cavitate at lower acoustic intensities (lower cavitation threshold) than degassed solutions. Dissolved CO2 and H2 in MES reactors (typical partial pressures 5-50 kPa) provide nucleation sites for cavitation, making bioelectrochemical reactors more susceptible to cavitation effects than pure water at any given frequency and intensity.
- **Biofilm mechanical properties**: The acoustic response of biofilm depends on its viscoelastic properties, which vary with age, composition, and environmental conditions. Young biofilms (less than 1 week) with low EPS content transmit acoustic energy with little attenuation. Mature biofilms (more than 4 weeks) with dense EPS matrix attenuate ultrasound by 5-20 dB/mm, absorbing acoustic energy and potentially converting it to localized heating. The resonant frequency of biofilm structures depends on thickness and elastic modulus, typically falling in the 10-1000 Hz range.

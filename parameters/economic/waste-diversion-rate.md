# Waste Diversion Rate

Waste diversion rate quantifies the fraction of waste material diverted from landfill or incineration through MES operation, expressed as a percentage. This includes: wastewater organic matter converted to electricity/hydrogen instead of producing sludge for disposal, sludge reduction relative to conventional treatment, and recovery of resources (nutrients, water) that would otherwise become waste. Waste diversion is a key circular economy metric valued by municipalities and ESG-focused investors.

MES achieve high waste diversion through three mechanisms: (1) direct conversion of organic carbon to electrical current/hydrogen rather than biomass (reducing sludge by 60--90% vs. aerobic treatment), (2) energy recovery from waste organic matter (diverting energy value from landfill), and (3) resource recovery (nutrients, clean water) from the waste stream. The effective waste diversion rate for MES treating domestic wastewater is typically 70--90% when accounting for all diverted material streams.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 0 -- 100%
- **MFC sludge diversion (vs. landfilling excess sludge)**: 60 -- 90%
- **Organic matter to energy (vs. dissipated as heat)**: 20 -- 60%
- **Water recovery (treated for reuse)**: 80 -- 95%
- **Nutrient recovery (struvite)**: 30 -- 70% of phosphorus
- **Overall waste diversion rate**: 70 -- 90%
- **Activated sludge (reference)**: 30 -- 50% (sludge to landfill)
- **Zero-waste target**: > 95%

## Measurement Methods

- **Mass Balance**: Total waste inputs (wastewater mass and composition) minus diverted outputs (electricity equivalent, hydrogen, reuse water, recovered nutrients) equals residual waste. Diversion rate = (1 - residual / input) * 100%.
- **Reporting Framework**: Diversion metrics calculated per GRI Standards (Global Reporting Initiative) or comparable frameworks for sustainability reporting.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Coulombic Efficiency**: Higher CE means more organic carbon converted to current (diverted from sludge).
- **Water Reuse**: Treating effluent to reuse quality diverts water from discharge.
- **Nutrient Recovery**: Struvite or ammonia recovery diverts nutrients from effluent.
- **Residual Sludge Management**: Composting or land application of remaining sludge counts as diversion from landfill.

# Nutrient Recovery

Nutrient recovery in microbial electrochemical systems refers to the efficiency with which nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium species are captured and concentrated from wastewater streams through bioelectrochemical processes, expressed as a percentage of influent nutrient mass or as a mass flux (g/m^2/d). MES offer unique mechanisms for nutrient recovery that are unavailable in conventional treatment: the electrochemical gradient across the membrane drives ion migration, enabling selective transport of ammonium (NH4+) from anode to cathode chambers, while localized alkaline conditions at the cathode can precipitate phosphorus as struvite (MgNH4PO4) or calcium phosphate minerals.

In microbial fuel cells and microbial electrolysis cells, the anodic oxidation of organic matter generates protons and releases ammonium from organic nitrogen compounds. The electric field established between anode and cathode drives cation migration through cation exchange membranes, transporting NH4+ ions into the cathode compartment where they accumulate at elevated concentrations. Simultaneously, the high pH generated by cathodic oxygen reduction or hydrogen evolution (pH 9-12) can volatilize ammonia for subsequent capture or precipitate phosphate minerals. Microbial desalination cells (MDCs) add a third chamber that further leverages ion transport for nutrient separation.

Nutrient recovery from MES is gaining importance as the circular economy paradigm transforms wastewater from a waste disposal problem into a resource recovery opportunity. Phosphorus recovery is particularly critical given the finite nature of phosphate rock reserves, with projections suggesting peak phosphorus within 50-100 years. MES-based nutrient recovery can simultaneously treat wastewater, generate energy, and produce fertilizer-grade nutrient products, creating a triple value proposition. At scale, the challenge lies in maintaining high recovery rates across varying influent compositions while managing membrane fouling and scaling.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 5% to 95% of influent nutrient
- **Nitrogen Recovery**: 40% to 80% in MFC/MEC with CEM
- **Phosphorus Recovery**: 20% to 90% as struvite or hydroxyapatite
- **Ammonium Flux**: 2 to 50 g N/m^2/d through cation exchange membrane
- **Phosphorus Precipitation**: 50 to 500 mg P/L recovered in cathode chamber
- **Potassium Co-recovery**: 15% to 60% alongside ammonium

## Measurement Methods

- **Mass Balance Method**: 1. Measure total nitrogen (TN), total phosphorus (TP), and potassium concentrations in influent, effluent, and any recovered product streams. 2. Use standard analytical methods: TN by persulfate digestion, TP by acid digestion and molybdate colorimetry, K by flame photometry or ICP-OES. 3. Calculate recovery efficiency: RE = (mass_recovered / mass_influent) * 100%. 4. Account for nutrient incorporation into biomass (measured as nutrient content of excess sludge). 5. Close the mass balance to within 10% to ensure measurement reliability.
- **Ion Flux Measurement**: 1. Measure ammonium, phosphate, and potassium concentrations in the cathode chamber at regular intervals. 2. Calculate the ion flux across the membrane: J = (C_cathode * V_cathode) / (A_membrane * t). 3. Normalize by current to obtain the transport number: fraction of charge carried by each ion species. 4. Higher transport numbers for target nutrients indicate better selectivity and recovery efficiency.
- **Product Characterization**: 1. Collect precipitated solids from the cathode chamber by filtration. 2. Characterize by XRD (crystal structure), ICP-OES (elemental composition), and SEM (morphology). 3. Quantify struvite purity and nutrient content to assess fertilizer value. 4. Calculate the mass of nutrient recovered in solid products relative to influent loading.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Current Density**: Higher current drives faster ion migration and greater ammonium transport. Current density of 1-10 A/m^2 provides significant NH4+ flux.
- **Membrane Type and Condition**: Cation exchange membranes (Nafion, CMI-7000) are essential for selective NH4+ transport. Fouling reduces selectivity and flux.
- **Cathode pH**: Alkaline cathode conditions (pH > 9) promote ammonia volatilization and phosphate mineral precipitation. pH control is critical for recovery optimization.
- **Influent Nutrient Concentrations**: Higher N and P concentrations in the influent increase driving forces for recovery. Municipal wastewater (30-60 mg N/L, 5-10 mg P/L) vs. urine (5000-9000 mg N/L) represent vastly different recovery scenarios.
- **Magnesium Availability**: Struvite precipitation requires Mg:N:P molar ratio near 1:1:1; Mg supplementation may be needed for phosphorus-rich wastewaters.
- **Competing Cations**: Na+, K+, and Ca2+ compete with NH4+ for membrane transport, reducing nitrogen recovery selectivity.
- **Temperature**: Affects both microbial activity and mineral precipitation kinetics. Lower temperatures reduce ammonia volatilization from the cathode.

## Related Parameters

- **name**: Nutrient Release (K)

- **relationship**: The rate of potassium release from wastewater substrates, which sets the supply rate for K recovery.
- **name**: Nutrient Release (N)

- **relationship**: Ammonification rate from organic nitrogen determines the NH4+ supply for electrochemical recovery.
- **name**: Nutrient Release (P)

- **relationship**: Phosphorus mineralization rate affects the dissolved phosphate available for precipitation.
- **name**: Salt Removal

- **relationship**: In MDCs, salt removal and nutrient recovery are coupled through ion transport mechanisms.
- **name**: Carbon Recovery

- **relationship**: Nutrient and carbon recovery compete for metabolic resources; optimizing both requires careful system design.

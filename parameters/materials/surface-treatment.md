# Electrode Surface Treatment

## Definition

Electrode surface treatment encompasses the physical, chemical, or biological modification methods applied to electrode surfaces to enhance biofilm attachment, increase electrochemical activity, improve wettability, or modify surface chemistry in microbial electrochemical systems (MES). Common treatments include acid oxidation, heat treatment, electrochemical activation, plasma treatment, and functionalization with conductive polymers or nanomaterials. Surface treatment is one of the most effective strategies for improving MES performance without changing the base electrode material.

## Typical Values

- **Acid Treatment**: HNO₃ (30-70%), H₂SO₄ (50-98%), or mixed acids; 1-24 hours
- **Heat Treatment**: 300-900°C in air or inert atmosphere; 1-4 hours
- **Ammonia Treatment**: NH₃ gas at 600-800°C; creates N-doped surfaces
- **Electrochemical Activation**: Cyclic voltammetry in H₂SO₄ (0.5 M), 50-100 cycles
- **Plasma Treatment**: O₂ or N₂ plasma, 1-30 minutes

**Treatment Effectiveness (Power Density Improvement)**:

- **Acid Oxidation**: 20-80% improvement
- **Heat Treatment**: 30-100% improvement
- **Ammonia Treatment**: 50-200% improvement
- **Polymer Coating (PANI, PPy)**: 100-300% improvement

## Measurement Methods

### Surface Characterization

1. **Contact Angle Measurement**:
   - Sessile drop method to assess hydrophilicity change
   - Treated electrodes typically show contact angle <30° (vs >90° untreated)

2. **X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)**:
   - Quantify surface functional groups (C-O, C=O, N-C)
   - Determine elemental composition changes

3. **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)**:
   - Visual assessment of surface morphology changes
   - Evaluate biofilm attachment after inoculation

## Affecting Factors

### Primary Factors

1. **Base Material**: Carbon felt responds differently than graphite plate; porous materials benefit more from acid treatment
2. **Treatment Duration**: Over-treatment can damage electrode structure and reduce conductivity
3. **Treatment Temperature**: Higher temperatures in heat treatment increase surface oxygen groups but can cause mass loss
4. **Atmosphere**: Inert vs. oxidizing atmosphere during heat treatment determines functional group types

## Performance Impact

Appropriate surface treatment can increase power density by 50-300% and reduce startup time from weeks to days. Nitrogen-doped surfaces created by ammonia treatment enhance biofilm attachment and direct electron transfer, with *Geobacter* colonization density increasing 2-5x. Treatment effects are most pronounced on carbon-based electrodes, where surface functional groups serve as electron transfer mediators.

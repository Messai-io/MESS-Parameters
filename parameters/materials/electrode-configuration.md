# Electrode Configuration

## Definition

Electrode configuration describes the geometric arrangement, orientation, and spatial relationship between anode and cathode electrodes within a microbial electrochemical system (MES). This includes parallel plate, concentric tube, brush-in-tube, stacked, and flow-through arrangements. The configuration directly impacts current distribution, mass transfer, internal resistance, and scalability of the system.

## Typical Values

- **Parallel Plate**: Electrode spacing 1-5 cm, most common lab configuration
- **Concentric Tube**: Inner/outer electrode radii ratio 0.3-0.7
- **Brush-in-Tube**: Carbon fiber brush anode centered in cylindrical cathode
- **Stacked/Series**: 2-10 cells in hydraulic or electrical series
- **Flow-Through**: Porous 3D electrodes with flow perpendicular to electrode surface

**Common Configurations by System Type**:

- **Single-Chamber MFC**: Brush anode + air cathode (most efficient)
- **Dual-Chamber MFC**: Parallel plate anode/cathode separated by membrane
- **Tubular MFC**: Concentric tube for continuous flow
- **MEC**: Close-spaced parallel plates to minimize ohmic losses

## Measurement Methods

### Characterization

1. **Geometric Measurements**:
   - Electrode-to-electrode distance (mm or cm)
   - Active surface area ratio (anode:cathode)
   - Volume-to-surface-area ratio

2. **Current Distribution Mapping**:
   - Multi-point potential measurements across electrode surfaces
   - Identify dead zones and current hotspots

## Affecting Factors

### Primary Factors

1. **Electrode Spacing**: Smaller spacing reduces ohmic losses but may cause short circuits or restrict biofilm growth
2. **Electrode Area Ratio**: Cathode-limited systems benefit from larger cathode area; anode area determines biofilm capacity
3. **Flow Pattern**: Determines substrate distribution and proton transport efficiency
4. **Scalability**: Stacked and tubular configurations scale better than single-chamber designs

## Performance Impact

Electrode configuration is a primary determinant of internal resistance. Reducing electrode spacing from 4 cm to 1 cm can increase power density by 2-3x. Brush-in-tube configurations achieve high surface-area-to-volume ratios (up to 9200 m²/m³), enabling current densities above 10 A/m². Proper configuration selection can reduce internal resistance from >100 ohms to <10 ohms.

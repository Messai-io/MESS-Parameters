# Ammonia Recovery

Ammonia recovery efficiency quantifies the fraction of ammoniacal nitrogen (NH3/NH4+) present in the influent stream that is captured and concentrated into a recoverable product stream within a microbial electrochemical system (MES). This parameter is expressed as a percentage and reflects the combined effectiveness of bioelectrochemical ammonium transport across ion exchange membranes, electrochemical concentration, and any downstream recovery processes (stripping, absorption, crystallization) integrated with the MES reactor.

MES technology enables ammonia recovery through a unique electrochemically-driven mechanism: ammonium ions (NH4+) migrate from the anode chamber through cation exchange membranes to the cathode chamber under the influence of the electrical current generated by or applied to the system. In the alkaline cathode environment (pH 9-12, generated by oxygen reduction or hydrogen evolution), ammonium is converted to volatile ammonia (NH3) which can be stripped from solution and captured in acid traps as ammonium sulfate ((NH4)2SO4), a marketable fertilizer. This electrochemical driving force distinguishes MES ammonia recovery from conventional biological nitrification-denitrification, which destroys nitrogen rather than recovering it.

The economic motivation for ammonia recovery from wastewater is substantial. Global ammonia production via the Haber-Bosch process consumes approximately 1.8% of global energy and produces 1.2% of global CO2 emissions. Recovering ammonia from wastewater as fertilizer offsets Haber-Bosch production, reduces wastewater nitrogen discharge, and generates a revenue-producing product that can improve MES economic viability. Energy consumption for electrochemical ammonia recovery (5-15 kWh/kg N) is competitive with conventional ammonia stripping (8-20 kWh/kg N) when the MES electrical energy contribution is considered.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 0% to 100%
- **Typical Range**: 30% to 85%
- **MFC with CEM (domestic wastewater)**: 30-60% NH4+ recovery
- **MFC with CEM (urine as substrate)**: 50-90% NH4+ recovery
- **MEC with ammonia stripping cathode**: 60-85%
- **Microbial desalination cell**: 40-70%
- **Electroconcentration MES**: 70-95%
- **Conventional nitrification-denitrification (for comparison)**: 0% recovery (nitrogen destroyed)
- **Ammonium transport rate across CEM**: 2-20 g N/m^2/d

## Measurement Methods

- **Nitrogen Mass Balance**: Influent and effluent ammoniacal nitrogen concentrations are measured by standard methods (Nessler method, phenate method, or ion-selective electrode per APHA 4500-NH3) and multiplied by respective flow rates. Nitrogen in all product streams (acid trap, concentrate, biomass) is quantified. Recovery = N in product / N in influent x 100%. Automated analysis using flow injection analyzers (Lachat QuikChem, +/- 2%) provides continuous monitoring capability.
- **Ion Chromatography**: NH4+ concentration in all liquid streams is measured by ion chromatography (e.g., Metrohm 930 Compact IC Flex, Thermo Dionex ICS-6000) with conductivity detection. This method simultaneously quantifies competing cations (Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+) to assess transport selectivity. Detection limit: 0.01 mg/L; precision: +/- 1-3%.
- **Ammonia Gas Sensor Monitoring**: For systems with ammonia stripping, continuous monitoring of NH3 concentration in the gas phase uses electrochemical gas sensors (Drager, City Technology) or tunable diode laser absorption spectroscopy (TDLAS). Combined with gas flow measurement, this provides real-time ammonia mass flux for instantaneous recovery rate calculation.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Current density**: Higher current drives faster ammonium migration across the CEM. At 10 A/m^2, typical NH4+ flux is 3-8 g N/m^2/d. At 50 A/m^2, flux increases to 15-40 g N/m^2/d but may be limited by back-diffusion and competing ion transport.
- **Influent ammonium concentration**: Higher concentrations provide a stronger concentration gradient driving diffusion-assisted transport. Urine (2,000-8,000 mg NH4-N/L) achieves 2-5x higher recovery rates than domestic wastewater (20-50 mg NH4-N/L).
- **Cathode pH**: Alkaline cathode conditions (pH > 9.3) shift the NH4+/NH3 equilibrium toward volatile NH3, enabling gas-phase stripping. Natural alkalinity generation at MES cathodes provides this pH without external chemical addition.
- **Membrane selectivity**: CEM selectivity for NH4+ over competing cations (Na+, K+) determines product purity. Nafion membranes show NH4+/Na+ selectivity of 1.2-1.8; specialized membranes achieve 2-5.
- **Hydraulic retention time**: Longer HRT provides more time for ammonium migration, increasing recovery. Optimal HRT for ammonia recovery is typically 12-48 hours, longer than HRT optimized for maximum power density (4-12 hours).

# Quantum Yield

Quantum yield in the context of microbial electrochemical systems refers to the efficiency of photon-to-electron or photon-to-product conversion in photo-assisted bioelectrochemical processes. This includes photo-bioelectrochemical cells where semiconductor photoanodes or photocathodes generate photo-excited charge carriers that participate in bioelectrochemical reactions, quantum dot-sensitized bioelectrodes, and bioluminescence-based biosensor systems. Quantum yield is defined as the number of desired events (electrons transferred, molecules produced, photons emitted) per photon absorbed, expressed as a percentage.

## Measurement Methods

- **1. **Chemical actinometry**: Use a chemical actinometer (ferrioxalate, Reinecke's salt) to measure the photon flux, then measure the product or electron yield per measured photon flux.**: 2. **Incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE)**: Measure photocurrent as a function of wavelength under monochromatic illumination. IPCE = (electrons/photon) x 100%. 3. **Integrating sphere measurement**: For fluorescence quantum yield, compare emission intensity with a known standard using an integrating sphere. 4. **Time-resolved spectroscopy**: Measure the rates of radiative and non-radiative decay to calculate quantum yield from lifetime data.

## Affecting Factors

- **text**: - **Wavelength**: Quantum yield varies with photon energy; most photoelectrodes have optimal wavelength ranges.
- **Light intensity**: At very high intensities, charge carrier recombination increases and quantum yield decreases.
- **Electrode-biofilm interface**: The coupling between photo-generated carriers and the biofilm electron transfer chain affects overall quantum yield.
- **Electrolyte composition**: Hole scavengers, electron mediators, and pH affect charge separation efficiency.
- **Temperature**: Higher temperatures increase non-radiative recombination, generally reducing quantum yield.
- **Semiconductor quality**: Defects, grain boundaries, and surface states act as recombination centers.

# Nutrient Release P

Nutrient release of phosphorus in microbial electrochemical systems refers to the rate and extent of conversion of particulate and organically-bound phosphorus into dissolved orthophosphate (PO4^3-) through microbial hydrolysis, enzymatic cleavage, and chemical dissolution processes occurring within the bioelectrochemical reactor. Expressed as mg P/L/d or as a percentage of total phosphorus solubilized, this parameter determines the amount of phosphorus available for electrochemical recovery or precipitation as valuable mineral fertilizers such as struvite (MgNH4PO4*6H2O) or hydroxyapatite (Ca5(PO4)3OH).

Phosphorus in wastewater exists in multiple forms: dissolved orthophosphate (readily available), dissolved organic phosphorus (requires enzymatic hydrolysis), and particulate phosphorus (requires dissolution). In the anaerobic anodic environment of MES, polyphosphate-accumulating organisms (PAOs) release stored intracellular polyphosphate as orthophosphate under anaerobic conditions, a mechanism well-known from enhanced biological phosphorus removal (EBPR) systems. Additionally, the acidic microenvironment generated by microbial organic acid production can dissolve calcium and iron phosphate precipitates, further increasing dissolved P availability.

The unique electrochemical environment of MES offers distinct advantages for phosphorus recovery. The alkaline conditions generated at the cathode (pH 9-12) from cathodic oxygen reduction or hydrogen evolution create thermodynamically favorable conditions for phosphate mineral precipitation without chemical addition. Dissolved phosphate migrating or diffusing from the anode compartment encounters the high-pH cathode zone and spontaneously precipitates as calcium phosphate or struvite if magnesium and ammonium are also present. This electrochemically-induced precipitation provides an elegant pathway for phosphorus recovery that is integral to MES operation rather than requiring a separate unit process.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 0 to 500 mg P/L/d
- **Municipal Wastewater**: 1 to 10 mg P/L/d release rate
- **Enhanced (with PAO activity)**: 5 to 30 mg P/L/d
- **Livestock Manure**: 10 to 100 mg P/L/d
- **Influent TP Range**: 3 to 15 mg P/L (municipal); 50 to 500 mg P/L (agricultural)
- **Orthophosphate Fraction**: 30% to 80% of TP after biological release

## Measurement Methods

- **Phosphorus Speciation Analysis**: 1. Measure total phosphorus (TP) in unfiltered samples using acid persulfate digestion followed by molybdate colorimetry (APHA 4500-P). 2. Measure dissolved reactive phosphorus (DRP/orthophosphate) in 0.45-micrometer filtered samples using the ascorbic acid molybdate method. 3. Calculate dissolved organic P: DOP = total dissolved P - DRP. 4. Calculate particulate P: PP = TP - total dissolved P. 5. Track the conversion of PP and DOP to DRP over time to determine the release rate.
- **Batch Release Tests**: 1. Prepare sealed anaerobic batch reactors with wastewater and anode biofilm-colonized electrode material. 2. Operate under MFC conditions (external circuit connected) and control conditions (open circuit). 3. Sample at regular intervals (0, 2, 4, 8, 12, 24 hours) for orthophosphate analysis. 4. Calculate the P release rate from the slope of orthophosphate accumulation over time. 5. Compare MFC and control to isolate the contribution of electrochemical activity to P release.
- **Precipitate Analysis**: 1. Collect precipitates from the cathode surface and catholyte by filtration and acid dissolution. 2. Analyze phosphorus content by ICP-OES after acid digestion. 3. Characterize mineral phase by X-ray diffraction (XRD) to confirm struvite, hydroxyapatite, or other phosphate minerals. 4. Calculate the rate of phosphorus sequestration in precipitates as an additional pathway for P release and recovery.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Redox Conditions**: Anaerobic conditions at the anode promote P release from PAOs and dissolution of iron-bound phosphates. Aerobic conditions favor P uptake.
- **pH Profile**: Acidic conditions near the anode dissolve calcium and iron phosphates; alkaline conditions at the cathode precipitate phosphate minerals.
- **Substrate Composition**: Wastewaters with high volatile fatty acid content stimulate PAO activity and P release. VFA/P ratio > 10 typically ensures complete P release.
- **Iron and Calcium Concentrations**: These cations bind phosphate, and their dissolution/precipitation behavior controls dissolved P availability.
- **Temperature**: P release from PAOs increases with temperature in the mesophilic range but declines above 37 degrees C.
- **Hydraulic Retention Time**: Longer HRT allows more complete hydrolysis of organic P and dissolution of particulate P.
- **Microbial Community**: Enrichment of PAOs (Accumulibacter, Tetrasphaera) in the anode biofilm enhances P release.

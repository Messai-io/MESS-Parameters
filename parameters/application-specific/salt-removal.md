# Salt Removal

Salt removal in microbial electrochemical systems, principally microbial desalination cells (MDCs), quantifies the mass or percentage of dissolved salts (primarily NaCl) removed from a saline water stream through bioelectrochemically-driven ion transport, expressed as mg/L reduction, percentage removal, or desalination rate (g NaCl/L/d). MDCs exploit the potential difference generated by anodic microbial oxidation of organic substrates and cathodic reduction reactions to drive ion migration through ion exchange membranes, achieving simultaneous wastewater treatment and water desalination without external energy input.

The mechanism of salt removal in MDCs relies on the electrochemical potential gradient established between the anode (where organic oxidation occurs) and cathode (where oxygen reduction or other cathodic reactions take place). Anions (Cl-) migrate from the central desalination chamber toward the anode through the anion exchange membrane, while cations (Na+) migrate toward the cathode through the cation exchange membrane. The magnitude of salt removal is governed by the current generated by the microbial fuel cell component, the number of ion exchange membrane pairs (in stacked configurations), the residence time in the desalination chamber, and the initial salinity of the feed water.

Scaling salt removal capacity from laboratory MDCs (milliliters) to practical desalination systems (cubic meters per day) is one of the most challenging aspects of MES technology development. Laboratory MDCs have demonstrated salt removal rates of 90-99% from brackish water (1-10 g/L TDS), but full desalination of seawater (35 g/L TDS) requires multiple stages or recirculation due to the limited current density and membrane area achievable in bioelectrochemical systems. Integration with reverse osmosis or electrodialysis as a pre-treatment step to reduce salinity load is a promising scale-up strategy.

## Typical Values

- **Valid Range**: 5% to 99% per pass
- **Brackish Water (1-5 g/L)**: 60% to 99% removal per pass
- **Seawater Pre-treatment**: 20% to 60% removal per pass
- **Desalination Rate**: 0.5 to 30 g NaCl/L/d for bench-scale MDCs
- **TDS Reduction**: 100 to 10,000 mg/L per pass depending on initial salinity
- **Multi-Stage Systems**: 90%+ cumulative removal from seawater

## Measurement Methods

- **Conductivity-Based Measurement**: 1. Measure the electrical conductivity of the desalination chamber influent and effluent using calibrated conductivity probes. 2. Convert conductivity to total dissolved solids (TDS) using an appropriate correlation factor (typically 0.5-0.7 for NaCl solutions). 3. Calculate salt removal percentage: SR = (TDS_in - TDS_out) / TDS_in * 100%. 4. For continuous operation, monitor conductivity continuously to track salt removal dynamics. 5. Calibrate the conductivity-TDS relationship for the specific water chemistry being treated.
- **Gravimetric Method**: 1. Collect known volumes of desalination chamber influent and effluent. 2. Evaporate to dryness at 103-105 degrees C following standard methods for total dissolved solids. 3. Weigh the residue to determine TDS concentration. 4. Calculate salt removal from the mass difference.
- **Ion Chromatography Analysis**: 1. Analyze specific ion concentrations (Na+, K+, Cl-, SO4^2-, etc.) in influent and effluent using ion chromatography. 2. Calculate individual ion removal percentages and total salt removal. 3. This method provides insight into selective ion transport through the membranes. 4. Monitor membrane selectivity degradation over time by tracking individual ion removal trends.

## Affecting Factors

### Primary

- **Current Density**: Ion transport is directly proportional to current (Faraday's law). Higher current densities achieve faster desalination rates. Typical MFC-driven current densities of 0.5-5 A/m^2 limit desalination speed.
- **Membrane Properties**: Ion exchange membrane selectivity, resistance, and area determine the ion flux capacity. Fouling degrades performance over time.
- **Initial Salinity**: Higher initial TDS provides greater driving force for ion transport but also increases osmotic backpressure and membrane resistance.
- **Hydraulic Retention Time**: Longer residence time in the desalination chamber allows more complete salt removal per pass but reduces volumetric throughput.
- **Number of Membrane Pairs**: Stacked MDC configurations with multiple AEM-CEM pairs can remove more salt per unit of charge transferred.
- **Temperature**: Higher temperature reduces solution viscosity and increases ionic mobility, improving desalination rate.
- **pH Effects**: Extreme pH at membrane surfaces can cause scaling (CaCO3, Mg(OH)2) that blocks ion transport channels.

## Related Parameters

- **name**: Nutrient Recovery

- **relationship**: Ion transport for desalination simultaneously moves nutrient ions (NH4+, K+, PO4^3-), coupling salt removal with nutrient recovery.
- **name**: Coulombic Efficiency

- **relationship**: Determines the current available to drive ion transport; higher CE provides more charge for desalination.
- **name**: Scale Factor

- **relationship**: Salt removal performance typically decreases during scale-up due to increased membrane area non-uniformity and flow distribution challenges.
- **name**: Energy Per Volume

- **relationship**: The energy cost per unit volume of desalinated water determines economic competitiveness with reverse osmosis.
- **name**: EC Adjustment

- **relationship**: Desalination changes the electrical conductivity of the treated water, which may require adjustment for downstream use.
